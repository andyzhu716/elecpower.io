# IPB vs NSPB: What’s the Difference?

When selecting an enclosed busbar system for a power plant or industrial project, one of the most common questions is whether to use **isolated phase bus (IPB)** or **non-segregated phase bus (NSPB)**.

## What Is IPB?

IPB is a metal-enclosed bus system in which each phase conductor is housed in its own separate metallic enclosure. It is most commonly used in generator outlet circuits and large utility power plant projects.

## What Is NSPB?

NSPB is a metal-enclosed bus system in which multiple phase conductors are arranged within a shared enclosure or in a less isolated structure than IPB. It is often used in auxiliary power systems and medium-voltage distribution.

## The Main Structural Difference

**IPB = separate enclosure for each phase**  
**NSPB = more compact shared / less isolated phase arrangement**

## Application Difference

### IPB applications
- Generator outlet circuits
- Generator to main transformer
- Generator-related branch systems

### NSPB applications
- Auxiliary power distribution
- Substations
- Industrial power systems
- Medium-voltage equipment connections

## Quick Comparison Table

| Item | IPB | NSPB |
|---|---|---|
| Phase Arrangement | Each phase separately enclosed | Multiple phases in shared / less isolated enclosure |
| Typical Use | Generator outlet circuits | Auxiliary and medium-voltage distribution |
| Current Duty | High-current applications | Distribution and auxiliary applications |
| Space Requirement | Larger | More compact |
| Common Projects | Large power plants | Auxiliary systems, substations, industrial plants |

## How to Choose Between IPB and NSPB

Choose **IPB** when the application is a generator outlet circuit with high current and strong phase isolation requirements.

Choose **NSPB** when the application is auxiliary or medium-voltage distribution and compact routing is important.

## Conclusion

IPB and NSPB are both important enclosed busbar solutions, but they serve different purposes. The right choice depends on electrical duty, layout conditions, and project application.
